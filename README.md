# Generative AI Insurance Underwriting Assistant

This project implements a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) based Generative AI solution designed to assist insurance underwriters in evaluating claims. By analyzing historical claims data and previous underwriter decisions, the AI assistant can provide precedent-based guidance on whether specific parts of a new claim should be accepted or rejected.

## 1. Knowledge Base (KB) and Sample Query Format

### Knowledge Base Format
The Knowledge Base consists of synthetic historical insurance claims data generated by `generate_synthetic_claims.py`. It includes 50 structured claims spanning various insurance lines (Commercial Auto, General Liability, Commercial Property, Workers Compensation, Cyber Liability, Commercial Crime, Professional Liability). 

Each claim in the KB (stored in CSV and JSON formats) contains the following fields:
- **Claim_ID**: Unique identifier for the claim (e.g., CLM-1000).
- **Industry**: The insured's industry sector (e.g., Retail, Construction).
- **Policy_Type**: The specific insurance policy in question.
- **Claim_Amount**: The total monetary value being claimed.
- **Incident_Description**: A natural language description of the event that led to the claim.
- **Accepted_Parts**: Specific elements of the claim that were approved.
- **Rejected_Parts**: Specific elements of the claim that were denied.
- **Expert_Decision**: High-level outcome (e.g., Full Accept, Partial Reject / Partial Accept, Full Reject).
- **Underwriter_Reasoning**: The detailed rationale provided by the expert underwriter explaining *why* certain parts were covered or excluded based on policy terms.

### Sample Query Format
Queries to the assistant are posed in natural language, describing a new or hypothetical claim scenario, asking for an underwriting assessment. For example:
- *"A powerful storm blew the roof off the retail building. Rain destroyed inventory. The insured also claims the cost to remove a fallen tree from the empty parking lot. What should we cover?"*
- *"Delivery van rear-ended a third-party vehicle. Our driver was off the clock and using the car for personal errands. Are we liable for the damages?"*
- *"Software deployed by the insured company caused a client's site to go down resulting in lost sales. They are suing for the lost sales and $10,000 in reputational harm."*

## 2. Overall GenAI Solution Design
The solution follows a classic RAG architecture implemented via LangChain:
1. **Data Generation & Ingestion**: `generate_synthetic_claims.py` creates the structured tabular historical data, converting it into rich-text documents where all fields are concatenated.
2. **Vector Space Indexing**: `build_index.py` processes the claims into LangChain `Document` objects. It embeds the text using OpenAI's `text-embedding-3-small` model and stores the document embeddings in a local Chroma vector database (`chroma_db/`).
3. **Retrieval**: When a query is made via `rag_bot.py`, the system fetches the top 4 semantic matches from Chroma using the query's embedding.
4. **Generation**: The retrieved historical claims are injected into the prompt context. The prompt is passed to OpenAI's `gpt-4o-mini` API, parameterized with `temperature=0` to maximize factual determinism, to generate the final underwriter assessment.

## 3. Prompts and RAG Effectiveness Enhancement
The system uses a highly constrained zero-shot prompt template designed to minimize hallucination and enforce reliance on historical context.

**Effectiveness Enhancements:**
- **Strict Grounding Instructs:** The prompt explicitly instructs: *"Do not make up coverage rules. Base your reasoning directly on the 'Underwriter Reasoning' and 'Accepted/Rejected Parts' found in the context."*
- **Refusal Mechanism:** It demands the LLM decline to answer if the context is insufficient, reducing the risk of "guessing" a policy exclusion (*"If the answer cannot be confidently derived from the provided context, state that you don't have enough historical precedent to make a ruling."*).
- **Semantic Metadata:** During indexing, claim traits (Industry, Policy Type) are clearly demarcated in the rich text, enabling the embedding model to draw stronger semantic parallels between new queries and historical cases of the same type.
- **Low Temperature:** Setting `temperature=0` restricts the model from generating overly creative narratives, sticking strictly to the retrieved facts.

## 4. Identification of Solution Limitations
- **Ocular Context Limits:** Attempting to retrieve too many precedents (k > 4) might dilute the most relevant information or exceed context windows. 
- **Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Queries:** If a user submits a claim fundamentally different from anything in the 50-claim KB, the vector search might retrieve the "least-inapplicable" claims. Although the prompt includes a refusal mechanism, semantic similarities in the vocabulary might occasionally trick the LLM into drawing false parallels.
- **Static Knowledge Base:** The Chroma database is currently static. When new precedential decisions are made by human underwriters, `build_index.py` must be explicitly re-run to update the KB.
- **Lack of Policy Document Integration:** The bot relies entirely on *past decisions* rather than reading the *actual policy contracts*, meaning it learns via case law (precedent) rather than statutory analysis.

## 5. Solution Using Any Platform
This solution is built openly using **Python 3**, **LangChain**, local **ChromaDB**, and **OpenAI's API**. It does not lock the user into an expensive, closed-ecosystem SaaS platform, enabling local vector data storage while leveraging state-of-the-art LLMs for reasoning.

## Analysis of Generative AI Helpfulness for Underwriting

### Can GenAI Help with this Task?
**(b) Mostly (80% or more)**
Generative AI can profoundly accelerate the underwriting workflow. Rather than manually digging through thousands of past claim files to find precedent for a niche scenario (e.g., *"Does our cyber policy cover upgraded security software post-breach?"*), a RAG system can instantly retrieve the exact historical precedent and draft a rationale. However, human underwriters are still necessary for the final 20% to review edge cases, interpret highly ambitious legalese in new policy forms, and make final subjective or high-stakes financial determinations.

### What Part of the Task Can GenAI Do and NOT Do?
**What GenAI CAN Do:**
- Instantly recall historical underwriting decisions.
- Summarize long claim incidents into structured data.
- Draft preliminary acceptance/rejection letters based clearly on precedent.
- Triage claims, automatically flagging blatantly non-covered incidents (e.g., intentional criminal acts) for quick rejection.

**What GenAI CANNOT Do:**
- Assess the physical reality or human intent underlying a claim (e.g., detecting nuanced insurance fraud via suspect behavior).
- Independently rewrite or interpret brand-new policy endorsements that have zero historical precedent.
- Bear legal or fiduciary responsibility for a wrongfully denied multi-million dollar claim.

### How to Build the GenAI Solution
The optimal approach is building **from scratch using Prompting and RAG** (as demonstrated in this codebase), rather than fine-tuning.
- **Why RAG over Fine-Tuning?** Insurance guidelines frequently change. If you fine-tune a model on past rules, updating its knowledge when a core policy changes is extremely difficult (catastrophic forgetting, hallucination retention). With RAG, you simply update the vector database with the new rules/claims, and the model instantly adapts. RAG also provides source citation (knowing exactly *which* past claim the AI based its decision on), which is critical for compliance and auditability.

### Foreseen Problems of Relying on GenAI vs. Humans
- **Hallucination of Policy Terms:** Without strict prompt guardrails, GenAI might invent a plausible-sounding but completely non-existent policy exclusion, potentially leading to bad faith lawsuits if a claim is wrongfully denied automatically.
- **Automation Bias:** Underwriters might become overly reliant on the bot, blindly approving its recommendations without applying critical thinking, leading to systemic errors if the KB contains a flawed precedent.
- **Lack of Empathy/Flexibility:** Human underwriters sometimes make "business decisions" to accept a borderline claim to retain a massively profitable client. An AI strictly interpreting precedent will rigidly deny it, potentially harming client relationships.
- **Regulatory Scrutiny:** Departments of Insurance heavily regulate how claims are handled. Fully automated AI denials might violate regulations requiring "reasonable human investigation" of a claim.
